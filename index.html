Abstract

Vaccination is among the most effective public health strategies for preventing infectious diseases and reducing morbidity and mortality worldwide. However, vaccine hesitancy—driven by misinformation, lack of awareness, and cultural or religious concerns—continues to pose a major challenge, including in Pakistan. University students represent a critical demographic as future healthcare consumers, community influencers, and potential healthcare workers. This study aims to assess the level of knowledge and attitudes toward vaccination among undergraduate students at Iqra University, Islamabad Campus. A cross-sectional survey design will be employed, targeting a sample of 50 students selected through convenience sampling. Data will be collected using a structured questionnaire administered via MS Excel. Descriptive statistics, including frequency distributions and percentages, will be computed and visualized through Pivot Tables and Pivot Chart Dashboards. Findings will be compiled into a PowerPoint presentation and integrated into a linked web page to enhance accessibility and dissemination. Results from this study are expected to inform targeted educational interventions aimed at improving vaccination awareness and acceptance among university students.

1. Introduction

Vaccination is one of the greatest public health achievements of the 20th and 21st centuries. Vaccines have led to the eradication of smallpox, the near elimination of polio, and a dramatic reduction in the incidence of diseases such as measles, hepatitis B, and meningitis. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), vaccines prevent 2 to 3 million deaths every year globally, and an additional 1.5 million deaths could be averted if vaccine coverage were improved worldwide.

Despite these proven benefits, vaccine hesitancy—defined as a delay in acceptance or refusal of vaccines despite the availability of vaccine services—has become a growing public health concern. The WHO listed vaccine hesitancy as one of the ten threats to global health in 2019. This phenomenon is influenced by a complex interplay of factors including complacency, lack of confidence in vaccines or healthcare providers, and barriers to access.

In Pakistan, the situation is particularly concerning. The country continues to report cases of vaccine-preventable diseases, partly due to low immunization coverage and persistent myths about vaccine safety and efficacy. Community-level awareness campaigns have yielded mixed results, highlighting the need to understand the knowledge base and attitudinal factors that influence vaccine uptake at the individual level.

University students occupy a unique position in society. As young adults transitioning into professional and civic roles, their perceptions about vaccination can shape broader community behavior. Moreover, students enrolled in health-related disciplines may become future advocates or detractors of vaccination programs. Understanding their current level of knowledge and attitudes is therefore crucial for designing targeted, evidence-based health communication strategies.

2. Problem Statement

Vaccine hesitancy among young adults, including university students, is a critical yet underexplored issue in Pakistan. While national immunization programs target children, the attitudes and knowledge of the emerging adult population regarding vaccination remain poorly documented. University students, particularly in urban settings like Islamabad, are regularly exposed to conflicting information via social media, peer networks, and conventional media, which may shape their perception of vaccines either positively or negatively.

Several specific problems motivate this study:

  • There is limited data on the vaccination-related knowledge levels of university students in Pakistan, particularly in Islamabad.
  • Misinformation and conspiracy theories about vaccines are rampant on digital platforms, and university students are frequent users of social media.
  • Negative attitudes toward vaccination among students may translate into vaccine avoidance not only for themselves but also in influencing younger siblings, relatives, and future generations.
  • There is a gap between the availability of vaccines and the willingness to accept them, and this gap is not well-studied in the student population.

3. Research Objectives

3.1 General Objective

To assess the knowledge and attitudes of undergraduate students at Iqra University, Islamabad Campus, toward vaccination.

3.2 Specific Objectives

  1. To determine the level of knowledge students have about vaccines, their mechanisms, types, and recommended schedules.
  2. To evaluate students' attitudes (positive, negative, or neutral) toward routine and COVID-19 vaccination.
  3. To identify common misconceptions or myths held by students regarding vaccine safety and efficacy.
  4. To explore the sources of vaccine-related information most commonly accessed by students (e.g., social media, healthcare providers, family).
  5. To examine the relationship between academic discipline (health vs. non-health programs) and vaccination knowledge/attitude.

4. Significance of the Study

This study carries both academic and practical significance:

Academic Contribution: The study will contribute to the limited body of literature on vaccine awareness among Pakistani university students. It will generate quantitative data that can be used as a baseline for future longitudinal or comparative studies.

Public Health Relevance: Understanding the specific gaps in vaccine knowledge and the nature of negative attitudes can help public health authorities and university administration design targeted, culturally sensitive health promotion programs.

Policy Implications: Results may provide evidence to support the integration of vaccine education into university curricula or orientation programs, thereby promoting a culture of proactive health behavior among students.

5. Methodology

A cross-sectional survey design will be used. This design is appropriate for this study as it allows the collection of data at a single point in time from a defined population, enabling the assessment of prevalence and associations without requiring long-term follow-up.

6. Ethical Considerations

Although this study does not involve clinical interventions, the following ethical guidelines will be observed:

  • Informed consent will be obtained from all participants prior to survey administration.
  • Participation will be entirely voluntary, with no academic penalty for non-participation.
  • Anonymity and confidentiality of respondents will be strictly maintained; no personally identifiable information will be collected.
  • Data will be stored securely and used solely for academic research purposes.

1. Background

Vaccination is one of the most effective public health interventions for preventing infectious diseases and reducing illness and death worldwide. Despite its proven effectiveness, vaccine hesitancy remains a significant public health concern due to misinformation, fear of side effects, religious misconceptions, and lack of awareness. University students are an important population because they influence their families and communities and represent future professionals. Understanding their knowledge and attitudes toward vaccination can help identify educational gaps and support awareness programs.

This project was conducted among undergraduate students of Iqra University, Islamabad Campus to evaluate their knowledge, perceptions, and attitudes regarding vaccination. Background and study rationale are based on the approved proposal.

2. Introduction

Vaccines protect individuals and communities by preventing infectious diseases and reducing disease transmission. Although vaccines have successfully controlled diseases such as polio, measles, hepatitis B, and COVID-19, misinformation has reduced public confidence in vaccination.

This project aimed to assess students' vaccination knowledge, beliefs regarding vaccine safety, COVID-19 vaccination status, sources of vaccine information, and willingness to recommend vaccination.

3. Problem Statement

There is limited information regarding the knowledge and attitudes of university students toward vaccination in Pakistan. Increasing misinformation through social media and misconceptions about vaccine safety may negatively affect vaccine acceptance. Understanding these attitudes can assist in planning future awareness campaigns and educational interventions.

4. Objectives

General Objective: To assess the knowledge and attitudes of undergraduate students toward vaccination.

Specific Objectives:

  • Assess students' knowledge regarding vaccines.
  • Evaluate attitudes toward vaccination.
  • Determine COVID-19 vaccination status.
  • Identify common sources of vaccine information.
  • Examine reasons behind vaccine hesitancy.
  • Assess willingness to recommend vaccination to others.

5. Methodology

The project was conducted using a cross-sectional descriptive survey layout. The target was 50 undergraduate students recruited using convenience sampling on the Iqra University Islamabad Campus. The survey form was designed and distributed electronically as a spreadsheet (Microsoft Excel) to streamline response capturing and database synchronization.

6. Ethical Approval

Ethical approval was obtained from the department committee before beginning the study. Participation was completely voluntary, confidentiality was maintained, and informed consent was obtained. No personal identifiers (like phone numbers, email addresses, or roll numbers) were collected or disclosed in the database sheet to ensure anonymity.

7. Key Findings Summary

Data Findings highlights (N = 50)

Routine Vaccination: 78% of the student body are fully vaccinated on their routine schedule.

COVID-19 Protection: 62% of respondents are fully vaccinated, while 12% are partially vaccinated.

Safety Beliefs: 98% of students strongly agree or agree that vaccines are safe. Only 2% disagreed.

Information Channels: Social media is the massive leader in information dissemination, accounting for 70% of where students get their information, followed by doctors (16%), family (8%), and news (6%).

Hesitancy Drivers: Fear of potential side effects is the leading contributor to hesitancy (90%), with only 10% stating they believe vaccines are simply not needed.

8. Conclusion

The study indicates that undergraduate students at Iqra University generally possess positive attitudes toward vaccination. Most respondents were vaccinated, believed vaccines are safe, and were willing to recommend vaccination to others. However, concerns regarding side effects continue to contribute to vaccine hesitancy. Since social media is the primary source of vaccine information, universities and healthcare professionals should utilize digital platforms to provide accurate, evidence-based information and counter misinformation. Educational campaigns and awareness programs can further improve vaccine acceptance among university students.

9. Recommendations

  1. Conduct regular vaccination awareness seminars in partnership with the IU Health Center.
  2. Increase involvement of pharmacy students and healthcare professionals in on-campus peer educational activities.
  3. Address vaccine myths and misinformation on social media through dedicated evidence-based university digital campaigns.
  4. Encourage students to obtain vaccine information from reliable health authorities like the WHO, NIH, and official government portals.
  5. Conduct larger multi-center studies involving multiple public and private universities in Pakistan.
